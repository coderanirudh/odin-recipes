Cacio e Pepe

Description

Cacio e Pepe (pronounced "ka-cho eh peh-peh") is a classic Roman pasta dish that translates literally to "cheese and pepper." It is the epitome of simple, elegant Italian cooking. The magic of this dish lies in its creamy, silky sauce, which is made without any cream or butter. Instead, it relies on the emulsion of starchy pasta water, finely grated Pecorino Romano cheese, and freshly cracked black pepper. It's a deeply savoury, peppery, and satisfying meal that proves you only need a few quality ingredients to create something spectacular.

Ingredients

  • Pasta
  • Pecorino Romano Cheese
  • Black Pepper
  • Salt

Recipe Instructions

  1. Boil Water:

    Bring a pot of water to a boil. Important Tip: Use slightly less water than you normally would, as this will make the pasta water extra starchy, which is key to the sauce. Once boiling, add a generous amount of salt.

  2. Cook Pasta:

    Add the spaghetti to the boiling water and cook according to package directions until it is al dente (still has a slight bite).

  3. Toast Pepper:

    While the pasta cooks, put the coarsely ground black pepper into a large, wide pan (large enough to hold the pasta) and toast it over medium heat for about 1 minute until it becomes very fragrant.

  4. Create the Sauce:

    Just before the pasta is done, scoop out about 1.5 cups of the starchy pasta water and set it aside. Drain the pasta, then immediately add it to the pan with the toasted pepper.

  5. Combine and Emulsify:

    Take the pan off the heat. Add about half of the reserved hot pasta water and all of the finely grated cheese. Using tongs, toss and stir the pasta vigorously. The heat from the pasta and water will melt the cheese, and the vigorous tossing will emulsify it into a creamy, smooth sauce that coats every strand.

  6. Adjust and Serve:

    If the sauce looks too thick, add another splash of the starchy pasta water until it reaches a silky consistency. Serve immediately in warm bowls, garnished with another sprinkle of cheese and a final crack of black pepper.
